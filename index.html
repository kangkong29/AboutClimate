The Rural Suite

Barrio & Lowland Dances

What is the Rural Suite?

The Rural Suite, also known as Barrio Dances, represents the beautiful simplicity of the Philippine provinces.

These dances are rooted in the heart of the common Filipino people, highlighting the joy found in hard work, nature, and community.

The Core Identity

The Spirit of the Barrio

Unlike the formal, Spanish-influenced Maria Clara suite, Rural dances do not focus on strict etiquette or high-society courtship.

Instead, they are celebrations of a bountiful harvest, a successful fishing trip, or simply the joy of being together after a long day of labor.

Suite Characteristics

Lively & Joyful

Movements are inherently happy, energetic, and playful, accompanied by wide smiles.

Labor Inspired

Choreography directly mimics everyday provincial tasks like planting or fishing.

Informal Setup

Highly accessible and less formal. Dancers often perform barefoot.

Fiesta Culture

Performed during town fiestas to celebrate a bountiful harvest and give thanks.

Connection to Nature

The props used in these dances reflect what is readily available in the Philippine environment.

Bamboo poles, coconut shells, wooden sticks, oil lamps, and clay pots are not just props—they are elements of the earth transformed into art and rhythm.

Theme 1

Farming Life

The Philippines is highly agricultural. Many rural dances show the cycle of planting, harvesting, and pounding rice.

These dances turn back-breaking labor into synchronized, rhythmic art forms to make the workday feel lighter.

Fishing Communities

With over 7,000 islands, coastal life is a major part of the Philippine rural experience.

Dances like Oasioas (using lighted lamps in cloths) originated from fishermen celebrating a good catch at sea.

The Town Fiesta

The Fiesta is the climax of rural life. Every barrio has a patron saint, and the fiesta is a time of immense celebration.

Rural dances take center stage during these events, bringing the entire community to the town plaza to watch, cheer, and dance.

Key Feature

Barefoot Performance

A defining characteristic of the Rural Suite is that dancers almost always perform barefoot.

This is not just for ease of movement; it represents a deep, physical connection to the soil and the earth that sustains them.

Wardrobe of the Barrio

Barong Tagalog / Camisa

Men wear lightweight, breathable shirts perfect for the tropical heat.

Salakot

Traditional wide-brimmed hats woven from rattan or reeds to block the sun.

Balintawak

Women wear colorful, informal dresses with butterfly sleeves and a panuelo (scarf).

Tapis

An overskirt or apron wrapped around the waist to protect the dress during work.

Spotlight Dance 1:

Tinikling

The National Dance

Tinikling is arguably the most internationally recognized Philippine folk dance.

It originated during the Spanish colonial era in the province of Leyte, Visayas.

The Inspiration

The Tikling Bird

The dance specifically imitates the movements of the tikling bird (a rail bird).

These birds were known to wander through tall grass and dodge the bamboo traps set by rice farmers to protect their crops.

The Bamboo Poles

Two bamboo poles are tapped against each other and against blocks of wood on the ground, creating a distinctive percussive rhythm.

The "clappers" must maintain a perfect beat, otherwise the dancers will get their ankles caught!

The Technique

Agility and Grace

Dancers must gracefully and quickly step in and out of the clapping bamboo poles.

As the music speeds up, the clapping becomes faster, demanding incredible agility, trust, and stamina from the performers.

The Rhythm (The Clappers)

Count 1

The clappers strike the bamboo poles against the wooden blocks on the floor.

Count 2

They strike the poles against the floor blocks a second time.

Count 3

The clappers lift the poles and strike them violently against each other.

The Dancer's Job

The dancer must have their feet OUT of the poles on Count 3!

The Rondalla Music

Tinikling is accompanied by a traditional string ensemble called the Rondalla.

Instruments like the bandurria, octavina, laud, and double bass create a bright, fast-paced melody that drives the dancers.

Fun Fact

Global Pop Culture

Tinikling has become a global phenomenon! It is frequently taught in physical education classes worldwide to improve coordination.

Modern dance crews even perform Tinikling to modern hip-hop and pop beats.

Spotlight Dance 2:

Maglalatik

The Mock Battle

Maglalatik is an indigenous dance from the Philippines in which coconut shell halves are attached to the dancers' hands and bodies.

It is uniquely performed entirely by male dancers.

The Origin

Biñan, Laguna

The dance originated in the town of Biñan in Laguna.

It is performed as an offering to San Isidro de Labrador, the patron saint of farmers, during the town fiesta.

The Story of Latik

The dance tells the story of a battle between the Moros and the Christians.

What are they fighting over? Latik—a delicious, sweet residue left behind after boiling coconut milk.

The Four Parts of Maglalatik

1. Baligtaran

The aggressive preparation and beginning of the mock combat.

2. Palipasan

The intense continuation of the fight between the two groups.

3. Paseo

The music shifts. This represents the reconciliation and truce.

4. Escaramusa

A joyous, fast-paced final dance to celebrate peace and friendship.

The Props

The Coconut Shells

Dancers wear 6-8 coconut shells attached to their shoulders, chest, back, thighs, and knees.

By striking the shells on their bodies and the shells of their partners, they act as their own percussion section, driving the entire beat of the dance.

Spotlight Dance 3:

Sakuting

Origin in Abra

Sakuting originated in the province of Abra, located in the northern part of the Philippines.

Like Maglalatik, it is a mock combat dance, but it uses wooden sticks instead of coconut shells.

The Tradition

A Christmas Dance

Traditionally, Sakuting was performed during the Christmas season.

Groups of young men and women would perform the dance from house to house in the town plaza, receiving "Aguinaldo" (gifts of money or food) in return.

The Wooden Sticks

Dancers use two wooden sticks, usually made of rattan, to rhythmically strike each other's sticks.

The choreography portrays the historical skirmishes between the Christian lowlanders and the non-Christian mountain tribes, ending in unity.

Bonus Dance

Itik-Itik

Another famous rural dance from Surigao! A young woman named Kanang was dancing at a baptismal party.

She spontaneously began imitating the movements of an "itik" (duck) wading in the mud and splashing water. The crowd loved it, and a new dance was born!

The Heart of the Philippines

The Rural Suite reminds us that true art and joy can be found in the simplest parts of everyday life.
